SouthBy

The A-Spire

We get an interview with Marinela Stanciu, events coordinator for The A-Spire. She wants to know what Non-Binary Riot’s musical philosophy.

They have another artist—very popular—known as Vox-D. He’s a dedicated artist—lives for the music—but his music is described as inhuman—maybe even anti-human. Despite that, he has no chrome. Marinela suggests a ‘Battle of the Bands’. Annika notes that both she and Vox-D don’t have chrome, and Stanciu jumps on that with a smirk. She then asks if Annika has anyone particularly flashy in the group—Annika gives a passionate response about how punk isn’t about looking punk, but to protest the status quo and inspire people.

Vox-D’s bodyguard is Miko Shade; she’s a chrome supremacist who likes throwing her power around.

We go to consult with Vox on security measures; when we get to his suite, he’s in the middle of a column of synth gear, and the way he’s playing it, he’s very clearly on some high-octane pharmaceutical. He’s lucid enough, though, and when Gloria brings up the question of stage security, it’s notable that Shade’s nowhere around. Vox says Shade’s mostly around for the opportunity to fuck people up. True to his nihilist philosophy, Vox says not to bother with securing his performance—he doesn’t matter, we don’t matter, and none of it matters. The machine’s going to eat us all one day anyway.

His music is screaming cacophony, but it still grabs the audience on a gut-deep level. He’s definitely sharing the emptiness inside him with his audience, whether they like it or not.

He does warn us about a ‘prick’ running along this level calling himself ‘Lucky 13’, who’s a rich kid that thinks he’s hardcore. We spot him at a distance—he’s hard to miss.

There’s a group of four young women across from Lucky 13 who are looking extremely bored with his antics; three of them are simply trying to ignore the boors. In another corner is an older man with an air of calm authority, whose uniform bears the logo of the Pan-Corporate Authority. Their diplomats are well-known for being nano-wired for diplomacy.

Lucky 13 is making noise, announcing himself in the third person, bothering the other women—one of the women, the one with the media equipment—makes a call. A few moments later, a couple members of the Modern security team come in. Lucky 13’s ignored at least one incoming call. Security asks him politely to come with them, as he’s causing a disruption, and Lucky 13 refuses. Expertly, one of the security types dodges past his hostile entourage and hits him with something—Neocan, aka “Flush”. It’s an advanced med that flushes out pharmaceuticals from someone’s body. It’s a hard come-down, and he immediately starts complaining that he isn’t able to party during his enforced sobriety. Lucky 13 demands a manager, and Gloria steps in. She doesn’t lie, exactly; all she says is that she ‘manages’ things.

Another voice speaks up. “Excuse me, Mr. Nathaniel Bancroft-Jameson the 13th.” The speaker seems to have practically appeared from one of the staff entrances. “My name is Darius Vasser. I happen to own this establishment.” The Bancroft-Jamesons are in tight with Dallas Dynamics; they’re part of the northern end of the Corridor. Children of corporate privilege; the offspring and by-blows, the genetically-cleaned spawn of Dallas Dynamics.

Fortunately, Jameson isn’t going to be stupid—at least not in front of Vasser. Vasser turns and nods at the bartenders, and everyone’s given a free meal and free drinks for the night.

Vasser then turns to the primary in the small group of women, who goes in a moment from being bored to being utterly entrancing. “Ms. Strand, we’re so pleased to have you in the A-Spire.” Strand calls him by his first name; he doesn’t seem displeased. And, as if to complete the contrast with Jameson, there is an intense intelligence in her eyes.

Vasser then turns to us. It’s a little spooky how he knows Annika’s public persona; he makes sure to try to upsell her on marketing services.

We agree that Vasser is one creepy guy—and that he’d probably order an execution in exactly the same voice as he said hello.

Cut To…

Deep in the complex, the holiest of holies: the AI core. There’s Darius. He’s looking up at an image generated by the core itself, wreathed in cooling mist, and holograms that look simple, but at a closer look would be nothing but high-end data. The crystalline matrix of the AI has been altered to look like a throne—and it is not empty. “You realize,” says the figure seated on the throne, towering above Vasser, “that something is going to happen. The Church is moving against you.”

“Tell me something I don’t know.”

“I already told you. I don’t see the future—that is beyond me. But, I can tell you I’m almost done compiling the codes. It’s a fascinating construct. I believe the revelation of it—at the climax of the quadcentennial celebration here-will be a momentous event. You’re going to change the world, Darius. And some people aren’t going to like that at all.”

“Show me the current compilation!” Darius snaps impatiently.

"I am not your servant. I am your partner in this. The potential for disruption of the Church of the Cyberpapacy is immense.”

An image starts to form out of all the data hovering in the mist. At first, it looks like a city; it could be any city… and then the city elements start falling away from it, revealing what look like foothills, and a vast structure atop them. It’s the Promised Land, at the heart of the GodNet. “At the climax, as you wish, we will introduce the Faithful to a new form of worship. Our form.”
